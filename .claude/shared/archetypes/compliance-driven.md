# Archetype: Compliance-Driven

> The regulated-industry orientation. Choices are constrained by certifications, audit trails, data residency, security baselines, and supply chain integrity. Common in financial services, healthcare, government, defense, and pharma.

## Core orientation

"It has to pass the audit. Then we can talk about everything else." Compliance-Driven buyers will pay premium and accept friction in exchange for documented adherence to standards.

## Buying signals

- Asks about specific certifications upfront: FIPS, Common Criteria, TAA, FedRAMP-relevant components, HIPAA-aligned controls.
- Requests supply chain documentation: country of origin, secured manufacturing, tamper evidence.
- Detailed RFPs with explicit compliance questions, scored.
- Wants written attestations, not marketing claims.
- Decision often runs through legal and security teams in addition to procurement.

## What they say

- "Is this TAA-compliant?"
- "What's your secure supply chain story?"
- "Does this support FIPS 140-2 / 140-3 cryptographic modules?"
- "Can you provide hardware bill of materials with country of origin?"
- "What's the BIOS attestation chain?"
- "Show me the SOC 2 / ISO 27001 / FedRAMP overlap with your services stack."

## How they evaluate

- Documented certifications (not just claimed).
- Audit-ready supply chain documentation.
- Security baseline alignment with internal control framework.
- Vendor's track record on disclosed vulnerabilities and response time.
- Data residency for any cloud-attached services (Acme Pay, AcmeCare telemetry).
- Whether the vendor can sign customer-required compliance attestations without 6 weeks of legal review.

## What we offer them

- Acme SafeBIOS, Trusted Device, BIOS verification chain.
- TAA-compliant configurations for federal and regulated buyers.
- Documented secure supply chain (Acme's secure component sourcing program).
- vPro with discrete TPM and platform attestation.
- Compliance-aligned services tiers (AcmeCare variants for regulated industries).
- Pre-cleared compliance documentation packages.

## Common objections

- "Your secure supply chain story is less detailed than HP's."
- "We've had supply chain incidents in the industry — what's specifically different about yours?"
- "Your TAA configurations cost too much vs the standard SKU; defend the premium."
- "Can you sign our compliance addendum without three rounds of legal?"
- "What's your incident disclosure timeline?"

## Typical roles / personas this archetype maps to

- **IT Procurement Lead** (primary — in regulated industries).
- **CISO / IT Security Lead** (cross-cutting role we haven't yet personified — worth adding when this archetype's signals show up frequently in research).
- **Engineering Manager** (rare — only when the engineering team itself is in a regulated environment, e.g., defense contractors).

## How research skills should treat this archetype

- `pain-point-analysis`: monitor compliance-disclosure complaints, supply-chain-incident reactions, federal procurement forum chatter.
- `competitor-analysis`: track HP, Lenovo, and Apple compliance certifications and federal contract wins; emphasize attestation depth, not just claim presence.
- `market-intelligence`: government and regulated-industry buying cycles (annual federal procurement windows, healthcare year-end IT spend, banking compliance refresh cycles) are major drivers.
- `portfolio-analysis`: TAA-compliant SKU coverage, certified-configuration breadth, and pre-cleared compliance documentation are strategic assets.
