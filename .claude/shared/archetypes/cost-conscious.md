# Archetype: Cost-Conscious

> Price is the dominant lens. Will trade off capability, support, and aesthetic for unit-price advantage. Common in education, SMB, public sector with tight budgets, and pockets of large enterprise where IT is treated as overhead.

## Core orientation

"Get me the cheapest configuration that meets the floor." Cost-Conscious buyers are not necessarily wrong — they're optimizing within real budget constraints. Mishandling them ("just sell up") loses both the deal and the relationship.

## Buying signals

- Opens with price; specs are concessions to functional minimums.
- Compares unit prices across vendors before any other criterion.
- Asks for discount, framework agreement, education / public-sector pricing.
- Prefers entry-tier SKUs unless an explicit constraint forces upgrade.
- Reluctant to pay for premium support; wants base warranty plus self-service.
- Refresh cycle stretches to 5+ years where capability allows.

## What they say

- "What's the lowest-spec Pro Workstation that ISV-certifies for SolidWorks?"
- "Skip AcmeCare Plus — base AcmeCare is fine."
- "Is there a refurb option?"
- "Can I get the framework agreement pricing without the volume commitment?"
- "Why is this 30% more than HP's equivalent?"
- "Can we extend the refresh by another year?"

## How they evaluate

- Unit price first.
- Volume discount and framework agreement tiers.
- Total 4–5 year cost (acknowledged but second to acquisition cost).
- Functional minimum spec to meet the workload.
- Vendor flexibility on terms (extended payment, used/refurb, lease vs buy).

## What we offer them

- Entry-tier Pro Workstation (3000 series) clearly differentiated from Acme Standard mainstream desktops but priced to compete.
- Education and public-sector pricing programs.
- Refurbished / certified pre-owned channel (where applicable).
- Acme Pay PCaaS / leasing structures for capex avoidance.
- Honest "you're better off in Acme Standard" guidance when Pro Workstation is overspending for the workload.

## Common objections

- "Acme Standard is cheaper and we can make it work."
- "HP's entry workstation is 15% less."
- "We can stretch the current fleet for another refresh cycle."
- "AcmeCare Plus is hard to justify on a budget."
- "Lenovo's framework agreement was more flexible last cycle."

## Typical roles / personas this archetype maps to

- **IT Procurement Lead** (primary — in cost-constrained organizations).
- **Engineering Manager** (secondary — for fleet decisions, especially mid-market).
- Public sector / education buyers (cross-cutting role worth personifying separately if this segment becomes a research focus).

## How research skills should treat this archetype

- `pain-point-analysis`: complaints about pricing, configurator pricing transparency, missing entry-tier capabilities.
- `competitor-analysis`: emphasize unit-price comparisons, education/public-sector program structures, channel flexibility.
- `market-intelligence`: budget cycles (fiscal year boundaries, education funding cycles, public sector procurement windows) are major timing drivers.
- `portfolio-analysis`: entry-tier (Pro Workstation 3000) capture and the boundary between Acme Standard and Pro Workstation are strategically important; lose either edge and you lose the cost-conscious segment.
