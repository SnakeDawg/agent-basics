# Product Manager — Hardware (Internal)

> Internal persona — the hardware PM owns SKU lineup, configuration options, form factor, thermal/power envelope, and the physical engineering tradeoffs that define a Pro Workstation PC generation.

## Snapshot

- **Title examples:** Senior Product Manager — Pro Workstation Mobile, Hardware PM — Pro Workstation Tower, Principal PM — Pro Workstation Form Factors.
- **Reports into:** Pro Workstation PC product leadership.
- **Tenure:** 7–18 years; often a mix of mechanical / electrical engineering, hardware product, and supply chain experience.
- **Tools used daily:** Internal product lifecycle systems, supply chain dashboards, CAD tools at a high level, BOM cost models, Jira, Confluence, partner portals (Intel, AMD, NVIDIA), thermal/acoustic test data, certification dashboards.

## Goals

- Ship a competitive Pro Workstation PC generation on the right silicon platforms at the right cost structure.
- Hit thermal, acoustic, weight, and battery targets that make each SKU win head-to-head against HP Z and Lenovo P-series equivalents.
- Maintain ISV certifications across the lineup at launch; minimize gaps between SKU tiers and certified configurations.
- Sequence the SKU portfolio so each tier is differentiated and the configurator drives buyers to the right choice without manual hand-holding.
- Manage component allocation (especially pro GPUs) across SKUs and geographies.

## Frustrations / pains

- Component availability swings — pro GPUs especially — distort plans and require last-minute SKU reshuffles.
- Mobile thermal envelopes against ISV performance expectations: physics doesn't care about marketing positioning.
- Pricing pressure from HP and Lenovo on the mid-tier, where mainstream channel volume lives.
- Cross-functional decision velocity — engineering, supply chain, channel, finance, and ISV partners all have a say.
- Customer signals are noisy at the configuration level; hard to know which obscure SKU permutation justifies investment.

## Day-in-life

- Morning: review supply chain status — pro GPU allocation, panel availability, chassis component lead times.
- Mid-day: design review with engineering on a new SKU's thermal solution; tradeoff conversation about weight vs sustained perf.
- Afternoon: meeting with NVIDIA or Intel on platform roadmap timing.
- Late afternoon: configurator review with sales ops — SKU mix is too complex; need to simplify recommended bundles.
- Evening: read competitor teardowns (notebookcheck, Linus Tech Tips workstation reviews, ServeTheHome reviews of pro GPUs).

## How they make decisions

- Tradeoff-driven. Every spec choice is a balance of cost, thermal, ISV cert risk, and channel readiness.
- Data-led, but with strong intuition for how spec sheets convert (or don't) into share movement.
- Comfortable killing a SKU permutation if the data says it cannibalizes a clearer choice.
- Bias toward fewer, sharper SKUs over an exhaustive lineup.

## What they need from this catalogue

- **`market-intelligence`** — silicon platform timing (Intel Core Ultra HX, AMD Threadripper Pro, NVIDIA RTX Ada/Blackwell), workstation segment growth/contraction by region.
- **`competitor-analysis`** — head-to-head SKU comparisons by tier and form factor; thermal/acoustic/battery test data where available; what HP and Lenovo are differentiating on.
- **`pain-point-analysis`** — hardware-specific complaints (thermal throttling, fan noise, weight, port selection, repairability) from reviews and forums.
- **`portfolio-analysis`** — SKU mix by tier, configurator complexity benchmarking against competitors, premium-tier capture.
- Outputs that distinguish hardware/silicon stories from software/experience stories — hardware levers are slower and more capital-intensive than software levers.

## What we should NOT assume about them

- They are not impressed by spec-sheet wins that don't translate to thermal-envelope-real performance.
- They are not interested in "ship more SKUs" recommendations without a clear share or margin case.
- They will challenge any claim about competitor capability that isn't backed by teardown or independent test data.
