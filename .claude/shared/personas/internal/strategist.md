# Strategist (Internal)

> Internal persona — the strategist is one of the primary users of agents in this catalogue. They reason about market direction, portfolio positioning, and multi-year choices.

## Snapshot

- **Title examples:** Director of Strategy, Senior Strategy Manager, Principal Strategist, VP of Strategic Planning.
- **Reports into:** Pro Workstation portfolio GM, CDG Commercial leadership, or a corporate strategy office.
- **Tenure:** Typically 8–20+ years experience; a mix of consulting backgrounds (BCG, McKinsey, Bain), in-house strategy rotations, and product line leadership.
- **Tools used daily:** PowerPoint / Google Slides, Excel, Tableau / Power BI, internal data warehouses, IDC / Gartner subscriptions, market intelligence platforms.

## Goals

- Shape multi-year strategy for the portfolio: where to play, how to win, what to invest in.
- Build defensible cases for executive forums and board reviews.
- Triangulate competitive moves, customer signals, and macro shifts into a coherent point of view.
- Identify share-gain opportunities and category risks before they show up in results.

## Frustrations / pains

- Internal data is fragmented across CRM, ERP, BI tools, and analyst portals; synthesis takes longer than analysis.
- Competitor intel is stale by the time it reaches a deck — most insight is reactive.
- Hard to separate signal from noise in customer feedback at scale.
- Pressure to "have a view" on every emergent trend (AI PC, sustainability, Apple Silicon impact) without the time to develop it deeply.
- Deck production consumes hours that should be spent on thinking.

## Day-in-life

- Morning: scan analyst notes, competitor news, internal dashboards.
- Mid-day: prep for a portfolio steering committee, refining one slide for the third time.
- Afternoon: review a market sizing built by a junior analyst, push back on assumptions.
- Late afternoon: 1:1 with the GM about whether to greenlight an investment in AI workstation positioning.
- Evening: read 2–3 long-form analyst reports.

## How they make decisions

- Frame the question first; data second. Insists on the "so what?" before the chart.
- Triangulates: analyst data + customer interviews + sales-team field intel + competitor public moves.
- Comfortable with directional answers when precise ones aren't possible; demands the assumptions be explicit.
- Trusts narrative arcs more than data dumps; expects synthesis, not just retrieval.

## What they need from this catalogue

- **`market-intelligence`** — fast synthesis of analyst commentary, share movements, and segment trends with explicit citation.
- **`competitor-analysis`** — structured side-by-side on HP Z, Lenovo P-series, Apple Silicon, BOXX/boutique; what they're saying, doing, and shipping.
- **`portfolio-analysis`** — Pro Workstation portfolio mix vs targets; gaps in the lineup; price-tier distribution.
- **`pain-point-analysis`** — synthesis of customer pain across reviews, forums, support tickets, NPS comments.
- All outputs should default to **hypothesis-led narrative + supporting evidence + explicit gaps**, not raw retrievals.

## What we should NOT assume about them

- They are not asking for the data; they are asking for the argument.
- They are not impressed by exhaustive lists; they are impressed by sharp framing.
- They will challenge any number whose source isn't visible.
